EXISTING ODOR MANAGEMENT SUMMARY  •   The system uses four primary lift stations equipped with tower biofilters and nitrate-based chemical injections specifically to suppress H2S formation during long forcemain transits. •   An Odour Management Facility (OMF) strips dissolved sulfides from wastewater using aeration and treats vapor-phase odors with a two-stage biofilter system to remove over 99% of H2S and associated odorants.  SRDRWC SYSTEM DYNAMICS AND ODOR CONTEXT  The South Red Deer Regional Wastewater system operates as a long, pressurized corridor, with wastewater traveling from Olds to Bowden, Innisfail, and Penhold before reaching the City of Red Deer WWTP. Typical residence times are prolonged (20-50h between each lift station) Each segment is lengthened in winter when infiltration is low. Such extended detention naturally drives oxygen depletion and sets the stage for anaerobic conditions. In practice, most lift stations cycle about every 20 minutes, so wet wells see frequent turnover while the force mains hold flow for days. Community complaints are not uniform; they concentrate along the W2–W3 corridor (Gasoline Alley), especially around the 30th Street manholes. SCADA trends indicate that H2S is not the dominant odor driver across the entire system; however, W3 is a notable exception, showing evening H2S spikes between roughly 8 p.m. and 4 a.m. that can reach ~15 ppm. Penhold only experiences very occasional, lower-level spikes (generally <5 ppm). Bowden and Innisfail continue to behave as FOG and industrial load “hotspots,” and Innisfail’s bio-rim tower is presently out of service. These are both factors that can reinforce septic conditions and contribute to nuisance odors even when H ⯑ S remains modest. The Penhold lift station demonstrates how targeted, data-driven control performs under this hydraulic reality. Using AI-guided Bioxide dosing keyed to flow and H ⯑ S, the OMF consistently delivers <1 ppm H ⯑ S at the outlet and has reportedly reduced chemical consumption by about 50%. Today, liquid-phase odor control relies primarily on nitrate (Bioxide) at all major lift stations, with typical consumption around 200L/day at each site. Operators generally adjust setpoints monthly and bias feed toward the night when travel times increase. In the collection system airspace, Odor Defender carbon “bio-bricks” are used in Gasoline Alley manholes; they are effective but require ongoing visits (roughly 4–6 man-hours total per six-month changeout cycle, spread over several weeks). Bug-on-a-Rope is deployed only in Waskasoo lift stations at present. Taken together, the data suggest a mixed-odor landscape: long travel and higher-strength/FOG inputs promote volatile fatty acids and other organics that can drive nuisance odors even when H2S is low, while W3’s nighttime behavior is clearly sulfide-driven. The OMF’s performance shows that when oxygenation/biofiltration is paired with real-time dosing logic, both odors and H2S can be held to very low levels without indiscriminate chemical use. Sampling Program Overview and Objectives  To support the proposed odor management strategy and provide SRDRWC with defensible, system- specific insight, ACC has worked with CEC to outline a short-term sampling program focused on understanding when, where, and why odor conditions develop within the South Regional collection system. The objective of this program is not regulatory compliance testing, but operational optimization. Specifically, the sampling is intended to: •   Confirm where odors are sulfide-driven versus non-H ⯑ S driven •   Identify upstream contributors that may be driving downstream odor complaints •   Support more targeted use of DaZZel Sewer Sweetener for non-sulfide odors •   Justify reactive, limited use of De-Sulph-A-Nator only where sulfide breakthrough is confirmed •   Reduce reliance on labor-intensive odor controls (e.g., bio-brick replacement) All options are structured around a six-week trial: •   ~3 weeks baseline monitoring (no ACC products) •   ~3 weeks monitoring during ACC product application  Existing Bioxide dosing would remain unchanged throughout the trial.  To ensure that sampling results are interpreted within the full operational context, it is recommended that SRDRWC maintain a concurrent odor complaint log and continue tracking available H ⯑ S sensor data during the trial period. Correlating analytical results with complaint frequency, sensor trends, and operator observations will strengthen confidence in any observed performance changes and support informed decisions regarding product placement and dosing going forward. PRODUCT DOSING STRATEGY  Where composite sampling is employed, the resulting time-weighted data provides an opportunity to incrementally adjust ACC product concentrations on a week-by-week basis during the trial period. By evaluating composite results alongside observed odor conditions, dosing can be cautiously refined to identify the lowest effective application rate that achieves odor precursor reduction and sulfide control objectives. This adaptive approach allows SRDRWC to balance performance with cost efficiency while maintaining stable system conditions. A similar dosing refinement model can be applied under a manual sampling framework if grab samples are consistently collected at the end of each monitoring week following product application. While this approach can still provide useful directional insight, it is inherently more sensitive to short- term variability and therefore represents a less reliable indicator of average system conditions than composite sampling.
