Yukon Territory/Yukon Territory   6  DISCUSSION   LOW TEMPERATURE BIOLOGICAL ACTIVITY  Biological activity in wastewater treatment plants can be affected by various factors, including temperature. In the winter, several key factors come into play that can impact the performance of biological processes in wastewater treatment: 1.   Reduced Microbial Activity : Cold temperatures can slow down the growth and activity of the bacteria and other microorganisms that are responsible for biodegrading organic pollutants in wastewater. This can result in longer retention times and reduced treatment performance. 2.   Temperature : Microbial activity is highly temperature-dependent. In colder winter months, the water temperature in wastewater treatment plants can drop significantly, which can slow down the metabolism of microorganisms responsible for breaking down organic matter in the wastewater. This can lead to reduced treatment efficiency. 3.   Sludge Accumulation : Cold temperatures can lead to increased sludge accumulation in treatment tanks, which may reduce the effective volume available for wastewater treatment. This can also result in lower treatment efficiency and increased maintenance requirements. 4.   Increased Energy Costs : To maintain a suitable temperature for biological processes during the winter, treatment plants may need to invest in heating systems, which can increase energy consumption and operational costs. To mitigate these issues, wastewater treatment plants in colder climates often employ various strategies, such as: 1.   Use of Cold-Tolerant Microbes : In some cases, treatment plants may use cold-tolerant microbial cultures that can remain active at lower temperatures. 2.   Adjusting Process Control : Operators may need to make adjustments to process control parameters, such as hydraulic retention time and mixed liquor suspended solids (MLSS) concentrations, to optimize treatment performance during winter. 3.   Insulation : Insulating treatment tanks and other infrastructure can help maintain a more stable temperature, reducing the impact of winter cold on biological activity. 4.   Heat Exchangers : Some plants use heat exchangers to capture and reuse heat generated during treatment processes to maintain a suitable temperature for microbial activity. 5.   Extended Aeration : Extending the aeration time in activated sludge processes can give microorganisms more time to break down organic matter, compensating for reduced metabolic rates in colder conditions. Overall, winter conditions can pose challenges to biological wastewater treatment processes. Still, with careful planning, operational adjustments, and appropriate technology, treatment plants can continue to treat wastewater during the colder months effectively.

Yukon Territory/Yukon Territory   7  DISCUSSION   RECOVERING FROM SYSTEM UPSET  Operators want to avoid a toxicity-induced wastewater system upset. It’s easy to understand why. A system upset comes with real problems like: •   a rise in pH •   an increase in TSS •   an upward spike in ammonia levels •   a drop in dissolved oxygen levels, and •   more sludge production For operators when you see the signs of a system upset your best response is to rebuild your healthy bacteria base. You can increase its vitality and recreate the conditions of a healthy and functioning process. WHAT IS THE CAUSE? More toxins in your influent are the top reason for a system upset. The chemicals designed to kill harmful bacteria in health-care, food production and home disinfection can make their way into the wastewater system. Once these toxic chemicals are in your wastewater influent, they continue to destroy both harmful and beneficial bacteria. Other events that trigger system upsets: •   a change in temperature, •   low food (offseason in a vacation town) or •   seasonal slug loads of fats, oils and grease (FOGs). HOW TO SOLVE A WASTEWATER SYSTEM UPSET? Once toxins have entered your wastewater system you will be able to see the visible signs of beneficial bacteria diminishing. Symptoms like less floc and an increase in TSS. The best response is to rebuild bacteria.

Food supplement products like dog food and chicken feed have traditionally been used to bolster BOD. These foods contain higher levels of fats and phosphorus than are beneficial to wastewater bacteria. They also contain high levels of inert solids which will accumulate in a wastewater treatment system and trap organic matter, leading to low DO conditions. Crude glycerin and molasses provide a food supplement in the form of sugar. While they can grow a population of bacteria quickly, they are not well equipped to digest anything other than sugar and provide little benefits digesting common municipal or industrial BOD. In fact, sugar based additives typically result in a cloudy effluent and polysaccharide slime. The human body cannot run on sugar alone and neither can a wastewater treatment plant. Traditional food supplements may offer a source of fuel, but they lack the nutrient balance and floc building components wastewater bacteria need.
